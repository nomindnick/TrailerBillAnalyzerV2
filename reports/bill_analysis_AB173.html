Bill Analysis Report

Bill Number: AB173

Chapter Number: 53

Title: An act to amend Sections 8594.14 and 13987 of, to add Sections 13986 and 14669.24 to, and to add and repeal Section 14108 of, the Government Code, to amend Section 75226 of the Public Resources Code, to amend Sections 187010, 187022, 187030, 187032, and 187034 of the Public Utilities Code, to amend Section 104.3 of, to add Section 2704.79 to, and to add and repeal Section 2196.3 of, the Streets and Highways Code, and to amend Section 15 of Chapter 71 of the Statutes of 2022, relating to transportation, and making an appropriation therefor, to take effect immediately, bill related to the budget.

Analysis by Practice Area

Board Governance

1. Affected Local Agencies

  • - Law enforcement agencies within cities and counties.

2. New Obligations

  • - Law enforcement agencies must now ensure they have used all available local resources before requesting an Ebony Alert.
  • - Agencies must determine that disseminating information to the public could assist in the recovery of the missing person.
  • - Example: A city police department may need to document efforts to utilize local resources such as community outreach or local media before requesting an Ebony Alert.

3. Funding, Operations, or Compliance

  • - Potential impact on local agency budgets due to the need for additional resources or training to meet the new requirements.
  • - Operational processes may need to be updated to include steps for resource utilization and public information dissemination.
  • - Compliance measures will need to be implemented to ensure all criteria are met before requesting an alert.

4. Interaction with State Agencies

  • - The change mandates the Department of the California Highway Patrol to activate the alert if criteria are met, ensuring more consistent state support.
  • - No new reporting requirements to state agencies are introduced, but local agencies must ensure their determinations align with state criteria.

5. Programs or Services

  • - The change may affect local law enforcement programs related to missing persons and community safety.
  • - Enhanced collaboration with state agencies could improve service delivery in missing person cases.

6. Key Points for Public Agency Clients

  • - Law enforcement agencies must exhaust local resources before requesting an Ebony Alert.
  • - The dissemination of public information is now a critical factor in the alert process.
  • - Agencies should review and possibly update their protocols for handling missing person cases.
  • - Training may be necessary to ensure compliance with the new requirements.

Conclusion

  • This change directly impacts local public agencies, particularly law enforcement, by imposing new procedural requirements and ensuring state support for Ebony Alerts. Agencies will need to adapt their operations to comply with these changes.

Business and Facilities

Practice Group Relevance (Primary): This change directly affects the business operations and facilities management of local agencies through impacts on infrastructure, procurement, or operational requirements.

1. Affected Local Agencies

  • - Cities
  • - Counties
  • - Special districts (particularly those involved in transportation infrastructure)

2. New Obligations

  • - Local agencies may need to coordinate with the Transportation Agency to secure funding for grade separation projects.
  • - Example: A city with a grade separation project at risk of losing funding may need to engage with the Transportation Agency to identify and secure alternative state funding.

3. Funding, Operations, or Compliance

  • - This change could impact local agency budgets by providing an opportunity to secure state funding for projects at risk of losing other funding sources.
  • - Operational processes may need to be adjusted to align with new funding application and reporting requirements.
  • - Compliance measures may include adhering to new reporting requirements to the Transportation Agency and ensuring project timelines are met to avoid funding reversion.

4. Interaction with State Agencies

  • - Local agencies may need to increase interactions with the Transportation Agency to identify and secure funding.
  • - New reporting requirements to the Transportation Agency may be introduced, particularly regarding project status and funding needs.

5. Programs or Services

  • - Grade separation projects, which are critical for improving transportation infrastructure and safety, may be directly impacted.
  • - Delays or loss of funding for these projects could affect public safety and traffic management services.

6. Key Points for Public Agency Clients

  • - Local agencies with grade separation projects should assess their current funding status and potential risks.
  • - Agencies should prepare to engage with the Transportation Agency to explore funding opportunities.
  • - Immediate actions may include reviewing project timelines and funding commitments to ensure compliance with new requirements.

Conclusion

  • This change directly impacts local public agencies by providing a mechanism to secure state funding for critical transportation projects at risk of losing other funding sources. It necessitates increased coordination with the Transportation Agency and may introduce new compliance and reporting obligations.
Practice Group Relevance (Primary): This change directly affects the business operations and facilities management of local agencies through impacts on infrastructure, procurement, or operational requirements.

1. Affected Local Agencies

  • - Regional transportation planning agencies
  • - Transit operators within cities, counties, and special districts

2. New Obligations

  • - The proposed change requires regional transportation planning agencies to submit updated regional short-term financial plans and transit operator data for the 2025–26 fiscal year and updated transit operator data for the 2026–27 and 2027–28 fiscal years.
  • - Example: A city transit agency will need to update its financial plans and data collection processes to comply with the new requirements to continue receiving funds.

3. Funding, Operations, or Compliance

  • - This change could impact local agency budgets by potentially altering the amount of funding received based on compliance with the new requirements.
  • - Operational processes related to financial planning and data collection/reporting will need to be updated to meet the new guidelines.
  • - New compliance measures include adhering to updated guidelines and submission deadlines set by the Transportation Agency.

4. Interaction with State Agencies

  • - The change introduces new reporting requirements to the Transportation Agency, necessitating more frequent updates and submissions.
  • - Local agencies may need to engage more closely with the Transportation Agency to ensure compliance and understand any guideline modifications.

5. Programs or Services

  • - The Transit and Intercity Rail Capital Program, which local agencies rely on for funding, will be directly impacted.
  • - Changes may affect the delivery of transit services if funding is contingent on compliance with new requirements.

6. Key Points for Public Agency Clients

  • - Local agencies must prepare to update financial plans and transit data for upcoming fiscal years to secure funding.
  • - Agencies should monitor guideline changes from the Transportation Agency closely.
  • - Immediate action includes reviewing current data collection and financial planning processes to ensure they align with new requirements.

Conclusion

  • The proposed change directly impacts local public agencies, particularly those involved in regional transportation planning and transit operations. The expansion of the accountability program and new reporting requirements necessitate operational and compliance adjustments to secure continued funding.
Practice Group Relevance (Primary): This change directly affects the business operations and facilities management of local agencies through impacts on infrastructure, procurement, or operational requirements.

1. Affected Local Agencies

  • - Cities
  • - Counties
  • - Special Districts
  • - Joint Powers Authorities

2. New Obligations

  • - The change does not introduce new obligations but alters the funding allocation, potentially affecting how local agencies plan and implement projects.
  • - Local agencies may need to adjust their project proposals to align with the new funding distribution, focusing more on implementation rather than planning.

3. Funding, Operations, or Compliance

  • - Local agency budgets may be impacted as more funds are available for implementation, potentially accelerating project timelines.
  • - Operational processes might need to be modified to prioritize implementation activities over planning.
  • - No new compliance measures are introduced, but agencies may need to adjust their project management strategies.

4. Interaction with State Agencies

  • - The change does not introduce new reporting requirements but may alter the nature of collaboration with the Department of Transportation, focusing more on implementation.
  • - Local agencies might receive more guidance on implementation strategies from state agencies.

5. Programs or Services

  • - Programs related to the transformation of highways into multimodal corridors will be directly impacted.
  • - The delivery of services to underserved communities might be expedited due to increased implementation funding.

6. Key Points for Public Agency Clients

  • - The funding allocation shift emphasizes implementation over planning.
  • - Local agencies should consider revising project proposals to maximize implementation funding.
  • - Agencies may need to enhance their capacity for project execution to utilize the increased implementation funds effectively.

Conclusion

  • The change directly impacts local public agencies by altering the funding allocation within the Reconnecting Communities: Highways to Boulevards Pilot Program, emphasizing implementation over planning. This requires local agencies to adjust their project strategies and potentially accelerates the delivery of services to underserved communities.
Practice Group Relevance (Primary): This change directly affects the business operations and facilities management of local agencies through impacts on infrastructure, procurement, or operational requirements.

1. Affected Local Agencies

  • - Cities and counties with port facilities
  • - Special districts involved in port operations
  • - Joint powers authorities managing port-related infrastructure

2. New Obligations

  • - Local agencies can apply for a letter of no prejudice to use their own funds upfront for projects, with the possibility of future reimbursement.
  • - Agencies must ensure compliance with conditions for reimbursement, which may require detailed financial tracking and reporting.
  • - Example: A city operating a port may need to allocate budget for initial project costs and establish processes for reimbursement claims.

3. Funding, Operations, or Compliance

  • - Budgets may be impacted as agencies need to front costs for projects, affecting cash flow and financial planning.
  • - Operational processes for project management and financial reporting may need adjustments to align with reimbursement requirements.
  • - Compliance measures will include ensuring expenditures meet eligibility criteria for reimbursement.

4. Interaction with State Agencies

  • - New interaction with the Secretary of Transportation for obtaining a letter of no prejudice.
  • - Potentially increased reporting and documentation requirements to secure reimbursement.

5. Programs or Services

  • - Port and Freight Infrastructure Program projects may be directly affected, impacting timelines and funding strategies.
  • - Changes could influence the speed and efficiency of goods movement improvements.

6. Key Points for Public Agency Clients

  • - Agencies can advance projects using their own funds with potential reimbursement.
  • - Careful financial management and compliance with program conditions are crucial.
  • - Agencies should evaluate cash flow impacts and prepare for detailed reporting.
  • - Consider strategic use of this option to accelerate high-priority projects.

Conclusion

  • This change directly impacts local public agencies by providing a mechanism to advance port-related projects with their own funds, subject to reimbursement. It introduces new financial and compliance responsibilities, affecting budgeting and operational processes.

Changes with No Local Agency Impact

This change affects only state-level agencies and operations, with no direct impact on local agencies.

Change:

  • The State Air Resources Board, pursuant to its authority to regulate motor vehicle emissions and emissions of greenhouse gases, has adopted the Advanced Clean Fleets Regulation, which imposes various requirements for transitioning local, state, and federal government fleets of medium- and heavy-duty trucks, other high-priority fleets of medium- and heavy-duty trucks, and drayage trucks to zero-emission vehicles.
  • Existing law, except as provided, beginning December 31, 2025, requires at least 15% of newly purchased vehicles with a gross vehicle weight rating of 19,000 pounds or more purchased by the Department of General Services and other state entities for the state vehicle fleet to be zero-emission vehicles. Existing law, except as provided, requires the Department of General Services, beginning no later than the 2024–25 fiscal year, to ensure that at least 50% of the
  • light-duty vehicles purchased for the state vehicle fleet each fiscal year are zero-emission vehicles.
  • This bill would require the Department of Transportation to annually compile and report information to the Legislature on or before October 1 of each year, beginning in 2025, regarding the zero-emission vehicles that the department purchases, owns, or leases. In each annual report, the bill would also require the department to conduct an analysis of the duty performance of the zero-emission vehicles that it acquires and to include information on its zero-emission vehicle charging and refueling stations, as specified. The bill would repeal these provisions on January 1, 2036.

This change affects only state-level agencies and operations, with no direct impact on local agencies.

Change:

  • Existing law generally authorizes the Director of General Services to hire,
  • lease, lease-purchase, or lease with the option to purchase real property for the use of a state agency, but prohibits the director from entering into a lease-purchase agreement for office space without specific legislative authorization.
  • This bill would authorize the Department of General Services, with the consent of the Department of Motor Vehicles, to enter into a lease-purchase agreement or lease with an option to purchase agreement for a build-to-suit office facility to replace the Department of Motor Vehicles field office in the City and County of San Francisco. The bill would authorize the project to be a mixed-use development that may include or integrate affordable housing. The bill would require the lease to be subject to approval by the Department of Finance, and subject to specified legislative notice requirements and the Property Acquisition Law.

This change affects only state-level agencies and operations, with no direct impact on local agencies.

Change:

  • Existing law creates the High-Speed Rail Authority to develop and implement a high-speed rail system in the state, with specified powers and duties. Existing law authorizes the High-Speed Rail Authority to enter into contracts with private or public entities for the design, construction, and operation of high-speed trains. Existing law creates the High-Speed Rail Authority Office of the Inspector General and prohibits the office from being a subdivision of any other governmental entity. Existing law authorizes the High-Speed Rail Authority Inspector General to initiate audits and reviews related to the delivery of the high-speed rail project and the selection and oversight of contractors, as provided. Existing law imposes other duties and responsibilities on the inspector general relating to the oversight of the High-Speed Rail Authority, including conducting audits and investigations relating to delivery of the high-speed rail project. Existing law prohibits the inspector general from
  • investigating personnel issues regarding employees of the High-Speed Rail Authority.
  • This bill would also impose on the inspector general the duty and responsibility to review or investigate adherence to contract provisions of any public or private entity that receives high-speed rail project funds. The bill would define “personnel issues” for purposes of the above-described prohibition. The bill would extend certain whistleblower protections applicable to employees of the High-Speed Rail Authority to employees of a contractor of the authority. The bill would require the High-Speed Rail Authority to ensure that specified provisions granting the inspector general access to records and property in connection with the inspector general’s authorized duties are included in all contracts and contract amendments executed on or after July 1, 2024, in which high-speed rail project funds will be expended. The bill would also revise the circumstances under which the Department
  • of Finance is required to notify the chairs of the budget committees of the Legislature regarding changes to the inspector general’s proposed budget.

This is a technical or administrative change that does not affect local agency operations or requirements.

Change:

  • Existing law, pursuant to the Safe, Reliable, High-Speed Passenger Train Bond Act for the 21st Century, authorizes $9,950,000,000 in general obligation bonds for high-speed rail development and other related purposes. The bond act requires the net proceeds received from the
  • sale of $9,000,000,000 principal amount of those bonds to be used, upon appropriation by the Legislature in the Budget Act, for planning and engineering for the high-speed train system and capital costs, as specified. The bond act also requires not more than 2.5% of those proceeds to be used for administrative purposes, and authorizes the Legislature, by statute, to increase the percentage of those proceeds used for administrative purposes up to a limit of not more than 5%. The bond act requires the amount of bond proceeds available for administrative purposes to be appropriated in the Budget Act.
  • This bill would increase the percentage of those bond proceeds available for administrative purposes by 2.5%, thereby raising the limit to not more than 5%.

This change does not create any new requirements, modify existing obligations, or directly affect the operations of local agencies.
