Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SECTION 1.

Summary:
This bill expands the criteria that law enforcement agencies must satisfy to request the activation of an Ebony Alert for missing Black youth. Specifically, the bill requires that before an alert is requested, the investigating agency must determine that all available local resources have been exhausted and that there exists actionable information that, if shared with the public, could help in safely recovering the missing individual. Local law enforcement agencies (such as city police departments and county sheriff’s offices) are affected because they are responsible for making these determinations and updating their investigative protocols to comply with the revised requirements.

Local Agencies Impacted

  • Local Law Enforcement Agencies

Impact Description:
Local Law Enforcement Agencies: Agencies must update their procedures to mandate the use of all available local resources and confirm the availability of actionable information before requesting an Ebony Alert. This change means revising internal protocols, enhancing documentation, and ensuring that the criteria are met prior to alert activation.

Key Action Items

  • Review and update local investigative protocols to incorporate the new criteria for requesting an Ebony Alert
  • Conduct training sessions for relevant personnel on the revised determinations and documentation requirements

Deadlines

  • 2027-07-01: Deadline for the California Highway Patrol to submit an evaluation report on the effectiveness, advantages, and impacts of the Ebony Alert system to the Governor’s office and the Legislature. (Affected agencies: California Highway Patrol)

Additional Requirements

  • Exhaust all available local investigative resources
  • Confirm there exists disseminable actionable information for the safe recovery of the missing person

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Municipal (primary) - This group provides legal counsel to cities, counties, and special districts, including local law enforcement agencies that must adapt to the new alert request criteria.
  • Investigations (secondary) - They help ensure that the updated investigative procedures and protocols meet legal requirements for initiating an Ebony Alert and managing missing person cases.

Summary:
This bill adds a new mandate that requires the Department of Transportation to compile and submit an annual report—starting in 2025—detailing its zero-emission vehicle acquisitions, duty performance metrics, and charging/refueling infrastructure. Although the Advanced Clean Fleets Regulation affects local, state, and federal government fleets, the new reporting obligation applies solely to the state-level DOT. Local public agencies such as cities, counties, school districts, special districts, and JPAs are not directly required to produce or submit similar reports, though they may use the DOT’s published data to guide their own fleet management and infrastructure planning.

Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 12.

Summary:
This bill revises the funding allocation for the Reconnecting Communities: Highways to Boulevards Pilot Program. Instead of mandating a fixed allocation of 25% of available funds for planning (with 75% for implementation), the bill now requires that up to 25% of available funds be set aside for planning, with the remainder directed to implementation. Local public agencies—such as local agencies, joint powers authorities, and transit agencies—are affected because they are among the eligible applicants for both planning and implementation grants under the program. They will need to reassess their project proposals, budgeting, and inter-agency coordination as the flexible allocation may alter the amount available for planning projects compared to the previous fixed standard.

  • Review and adjust internal budgeting and grant application processes to accommodate potential changes in the planning fund percentage.

Summary:
This bill amends the Port and Freight Infrastructure Program by authorizing lead applicant agencies to apply for a letter of no prejudice from the Secretary of Transportation. This change affects local public agencies such as port authorities, local transportation agencies, and special districts that administer or operate eligible port projects because they now have the option to fund projects using their own money with an opportunity for later state reimbursement. As a result, these agencies gain increased financial flexibility to initiate port-specific projects, but they must meet specific conditions—including project commencement, eligible expenditures, and compliance with legal standards like CEQA—before reimbursement is approved.

  • Lead Applicant Agencies (Port Authorities, Local Transportation Agencies, Special Districts)

Impact Description:
Lead Applicant Agencies (Port Authorities, Local Transportation Agencies, Special Districts): Allows these agencies to expend their own funds upfront on port-specific projects and later seek reimbursement, subject to meeting conditions regarding project commencement, eligibility of expenditures, and statutory compliance. (Deadline: June 30, 2033)

Key Action Items

  • Assess eligibility and prepare application for a letter of no prejudice from the Secretary of Transportation
  • Review and update internal financing and compliance protocols to align with the new reimbursement and project initiation requirements

Deadlines

  • 2034-01-01: The funding mechanism provision is repealed entirely, terminating the reimbursement option (Affected agencies: Lead Applicant Agencies, Port Authorities, Local Transportation Agencies, Special Districts)

  • Expenditures must be eligible for reimbursement under applicable laws and procedures
