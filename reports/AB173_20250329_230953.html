Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 2.

Summary:
The legislative change revises state transportation funding processes to support grade separation projects that are at risk of losing federal and local funding or facing schedule delays. It requires the Secretary of Transportation to coordinate with the Department of Transportation and the California Transportation Commission to identify available funding and to potentially reallocate that funding directly to these projects, with a report due to the Legislature by April 30, 2025. Although the primary mechanism is state-driven, local agencies involved in transportation projects may be indirectly affected through changes in funding commitments, program participation, and project scheduling.

Local Agencies Impacted

  • City
  • County
  • Special District
  • Joint Powers Authority

Impact Description:
City: Cities involved in local transportation and grade separation projects may see changes in funding flows and matching fund requirements as the state reallocates money. This could affect local budgeting, project timelines, and coordination with state agencies. County: Counties partnering in transportation improvement projects may be impacted if state reallocation of funds alters local funding contributions or matching requirements. Counties will need to adjust plans and possibly re-evaluate local funding strategies. Special District: Special Districts responsible for specific transportation services might encounter adjustments in funding allocations that influence their capital improvement plans, requiring updates to project schedules or matching contributions. Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities that facilitate interagency transportation projects may need to renegotiate cost-sharing arrangements if state reallocated funds reduce the need for local contributions or alter expected funding formulas.

Key Action Items

  • Local agencies should closely monitor communications from the Secretary of Transportation and state agencies regarding funding allocations for grade separation projects.
  • Coordinate with state transportation authorities to assess the impact on existing local funding commitments and project timelines.
  • Review and adjust local budgets, matching fund strategies, and project schedules in anticipation of potential state reallocation.
  • Prepare to engage in negotiations or revisions of cost-sharing agreements if the funding formula or allocations change.

Deadlines

  • 2025-04-30: Deadline for the Secretary of Transportation to report to the Legislature on the funding identified for grade separation projects. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)

Additional Requirements

  • Coordinate with the Department of Transportation and the California Transportation Commission to identify available state funding.
  • Reassess local funding commitments and matching fund arrangements in response to state reallocation.
  • Establish internal protocols for monitoring state funding developments and legislative reporting.
  • Adjust project planning and schedules to account for potential funding-related delays.

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Municipal (primary) - Municipal practice is critical as cities and counties are directly involved in local transportation projects and must navigate changes in funding and coordination resulting from the state reallocation.
  • Public Finance (primary) - Public Finance is integral to understanding and managing the shifts in funding streams and allocation methodologies impacting grade separation projects and local funding commitments.

Summary:
The legislative change expands the Transportation Agency’s accountability program to include funds from the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund for the formula-based Transit and Intercity Rail Capital Program. As a result, regional transportation planning agencies – and by extension the local governmental bodies that participate in or are affiliated with these agencies – face new compliance requirements for submitting updated regional short‐term financial plans and transit operator data. These changes impact local entities involved in transit planning and funding, requiring them to adjust their internal processes to meet the revised reporting and funding guidelines.

  • County
  • Special District
  • Joint Powers Authority

They may need to coordinate with regional agencies to update and submit financial plans and transit operator data in order to secure funding under the expanded accountability program. County: Counties involved in regional transportation planning or that host local transit operations must comply with new submission requirements. They will need to update their regional short‐term financial plans and transit operator data to continue accessing appropriated funds from both the General Fund and the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund. Special District: Special Districts, particularly those managing transit services or infrastructure projects, will be impacted by modifications to the funding accountability guidelines. They must adjust reporting practices to meet the new data submission requirements to remain eligible for funding. Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities that manage multi-jurisdictional transit and transportation projects will need to implement the updated reporting requirements. This includes submitting updated financial information and transit data, ensuring that all member agencies are coordinated to comply with the new accountability program.

  • Coordinate with regional transportation planning agencies to ensure local reporting aligns with state-modified guidelines.

  • Adjust funding application and reporting procedures in advance of the 2025–26, 2026–27, and 2027–28 fiscal year requirements.

Summary:
The legislative change enacts the Advanced Clean Fleets Regulation, which, under the authority of the State Air Resources Board, mandates the transition of government fleets to zero‐emission vehicles. While the regulation explicitly sets reporting and procurement milestones for state entities, its scope includes local government fleets as well. This means that cities, counties, special districts, and joint powers authorities may need to adjust their fleet management, procurement practices, and infrastructure planning—even if indirectly—to align with the new zero-emission requirements and evolving state reporting protocols.

Local Agencies Impacted

  • City
  • County
  • Special District
  • Joint Powers Authority

Impact Description:
City: Cities that operate fleets of medium- and heavy-duty trucks (e.g., for public works, maintenance, and other municipal services) must prepare to transition a portion of new vehicle acquisitions to zero-emission models. They will need to review their fleet composition, update procurement policies, and plan for the installation of EV charging/refueling infrastructure in alignment with emerging state regulations. (Deadline: December 31, 2025) County: Counties, as providers of various public services, also operate fleets that may include medium- and heavy-duty trucks. They must assess their vehicle inventory, update purchasing practices, and potentially invest in charging and refueling infrastructure to meet the impending zero-emission transition requirements. (Deadline: December 31, 2025) Special District: Special districts that manage specific public services (such as water, sanitation, or fire services) and operate truck fleets are indirectly affected by the regulation. They will need to align their vehicle acquisition and infrastructure planning with the state’s clean fleet goals. (Deadline: December 31, 2025) Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities, which are formed by multiple public agencies to share services, may be impacted if they operate fleet vehicles. They should coordinate across member agencies to ensure that fleet modernization efforts—including the procurement of zero-emission vehicles and deployment of charging/refueling stations—are executed in a timely fashion. (Deadline: December 31, 2025)

Key Action Items

  • Conduct a comprehensive review of current fleet composition and identify medium- and heavy-duty vehicles subject to the regulation.
  • Update procurement policies to prioritize acquiring zero-emission vehicles in future purchases.
  • Develop a detailed plan for installing and maintaining EV charging/refueling stations to support fleet operations.
  • Coordinate with state agencies, particularly the Department of Transportation, to monitor reporting requirements and guidance.
  • Explore and secure funding sources to support the upfront capital expenditures associated with the fleet transition.

Deadlines

  • 2025-12-31: Anticipated compliance deadline by which local agencies should begin transitioning new medium- and heavy-duty vehicle acquisitions to zero-emission models, aligning with similar state mandates. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)
  • 2036-01-01: Deadline for the repeal of the Department of Transportation's annual reporting provisions on zero-emission vehicle performance, which may indirectly alter state guidance affecting local fleet policies.

Additional Requirements

  • Transition a set percentage of new fleet vehicle purchases to zero-emission models.
  • Incorporate EV charging and refueling infrastructure in agency operational plans.
  • Revise fleet procurement and management policies to comply with Advanced Clean Fleets Regulation standards.
  • Monitor ongoing state reporting and analysis for potential adjustments in compliance requirements.

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Municipal (primary) - Municipal counsel is directly involved in advising cities, counties, and similar local agencies on compliance, regulatory changes, and governance issues arising from new fleet transition requirements.
  • Facilities and Business (secondary) - This group assists agencies with business operations and facility-related challenges, including updating procurement processes and planning for EV charging/refueling infrastructure modifications.
  • Public Finance (secondary) - Public Finance practitioners can provide guidance on budgeting, funding options, and financial planning for the capital expenditures related to fleet upgrades and necessary infrastructure enhancements.

They should also prepare for increased audit and review processes by the Inspector General. (Deadline: July 01, 2024) Special District: Special Districts involved in high-speed rail projects must ensure that all contracts funded by high-speed rail monies include provisions for Inspector General access. They will also need to update oversight practices to align with the new audit and investigation mandates. Instead of a fixed 25% of funds dedicated to planning and 75% to implementation, the new law requires that up to 25% of available funds be reserved for planning, with the remaining available for implementation. County: Counties, as major local government entities involved in transportation projects, must adjust their application and financial management processes to reflect the new division of funds. This change may affect project planning and require coordination with state DOT updates. Special District: Special Districts that participate in or partner on projects under the pilot program will need to review and update their compliance and fiscal management practices due to the modified planning versus implementation funding split. Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities, formed by multiple local agencies to manage shared projects, will be impacted by the new funding allocation guidelines. They must ensure that collaborative grant proposals and planning documents accurately reflect the potential for less than a fixed 25% planning allocation.

Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 13.

Summary:
This legislative change authorizes lead applicant agencies under the Port and Freight Infrastructure Program to apply for a letter of no prejudice. With this letter, a lead applicant—such as a city, county, special district, or joint powers authority—may use its own funds to invest in approved port infrastructure projects and later claim reimbursement from program moneys, subject to meeting specific eligibility conditions. The provision is temporary, becoming inoperative on 2033-06-30 and repealed on 2034-01-01, requiring affected agencies to adjust their funding, budgeting, and compliance processes accordingly.

Local Agencies Impacted

  • City
  • County
  • Special District
  • Joint Powers Authority

Impact Description:
City: Cities acting as lead applicant agencies for port infrastructure projects can now expend their own funds after securing a letter of no prejudice from the Secretary of Transportation. They must implement new budgeting and compliance measures to track eligible expenditures to qualify for future reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) County: Counties eligible to be lead applicants under the program may deploy local funds on approved port projects. They are required to follow the new application process and establish financial tracking systems to document expenditures for later reimbursement as mandated by the state. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) Special District: Special Districts that manage or operate port-related projects now have the opportunity to act as lead applicant agencies. They must apply for the letter of no prejudice and ensure that all eligible project expenditures are documented properly for future reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities, formed through agreements among local agencies, can now coordinate as lead applicants to utilize their own funds for port infrastructure projects. They need to work collaboratively to submit the required application and establish shared systems for expense tracking in order to secure future reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033)

Key Action Items

  • Review eligibility criteria and guidelines for acting as a lead applicant under the Port and Freight Infrastructure Program.
  • Develop or update internal financial tracking and compliance systems to document all eligible expenditures.
  • Submit an application for a letter of no prejudice before the inoperative deadline of 2033-06-30.

Deadlines

  • 2033-06-30: Deadline by which affected agencies must submit applications to leverage the self-funding provision; after this date, the provision becomes inoperative. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)
  • 2034-01-01: Date when the new funding mechanism provisions are repealed, ending any possibility for reimbursement under this bill. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)

Additional Requirements

  • Application for the letter of no prejudice must adhere to state-established guidelines.
  • Agencies must maintain detailed records of all eligible project expenditures for future reimbursement.
  • Compliance with eligibility conditions is required to secure reimbursement from program funds.
  • Agencies must monitor upcoming deadlines to avoid loss of access to the funding mechanism.

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Municipal (primary) - Cities, counties, special districts, and joint powers authorities are directly impacted by the new funding mechanism and require guidance on compliance and governance issues.
  • Public Finance (primary) - The introduction of a self-funding and reimbursement process directly affects budgeting and financial oversight, making public finance counsel crucial for affected agencies.
  • Facilities and Business (secondary) - Given the infrastructure focus of port projects, agencies may need advice on managing project-related operational changes and business practices under the new provisions.
