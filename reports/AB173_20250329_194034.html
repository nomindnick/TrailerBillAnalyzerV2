Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 2.

Summary:
This legislative change directs the Secretary of Transportation to work with the Department of Transportation and the California Transportation Commission to review state transportation programs for available funding that can support grade separation projects at risk of losing federal or local funding commitments or facing schedule delays. The Secretary must report the identified funding to the Legislature by April 30, 2025.

Local Agencies Impacted

No local agencies identified.

Impact Description:
No direct impact on local agencies.

Key Action Items

  • Local public agencies with grade separation projects should review their current funding commitments and monitor state communications regarding potential supplemental funding.
  • Where applicable, local officials should engage with state transportation representatives to clarify eligibility criteria and the process for direct allocation of funds in cases of local funding shortfalls.

Deadlines

  • 2025-04-30: Deadline for the Secretary of Transportation to report to the Legislature on the funding identified for at-risk grade separation projects.

Additional Requirements

  • Coordinate with the Department of Transportation and the California Transportation Commission to identify available state transportation funds.
  • Prioritize and, where appropriate, directly allocate funding to support grade separation projects that are at risk of losing or failing to secure federal and local funding commitments or encountering project schedule delays.

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Public Finance (primary) - The change centers on finding and reallocating state funds from transportation programs, a matter that directly relates to budgeting and finance.
  • Municipal (primary) - While the bill operates at the state level, local governments (cities, counties, and special districts) that sponsor grade separation projects may be indirectly affected by shifts in funding sources.
Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 13.

Summary:
This legislative change under the Port and Freight Infrastructure Program allows a lead applicant agency to request a letter of no prejudice from the Secretary of Transportation, permitting the agency to use its own funds on port-specific projects with the expectation of future reimbursement if certain conditions are met. The provision is temporary, becoming inoperative on 2033-06-30 and repealed on 2034-01-01, which requires affected local public agencies to act promptly if they wish to leverage this financing tool.

Local Agencies Impacted

  • City
  • County
  • Special District
  • Joint Powers Authority

Impact Description:
City: Cities operating port terminals or involved in port-specific projects can apply for a letter of no prejudice that allows them to expend their own funds upfront, subject to later reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) County: Counties that administer eligible port projects now have the option to use their own funds early by applying for a letter of no prejudice, with reimbursement available if conditions are met. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) Special District: Special districts involved in port-specific projects may take advantage of the new funding provision to utilize their own funds, pending future reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033) Joint Powers Authority: Joint Powers Authorities serving as lead applicant agencies in port projects can now expend their own money after securing a letter of no prejudice, subject to conditions for later reimbursement. (Deadline: June 30, 2033)

Key Action Items

  • Review eligibility criteria and determine if the agency qualifies as a lead applicant under the program
  • Submit an application for a letter of no prejudice before 2033-06-30
  • Establish protocols to ensure compliance with future reimbursement conditions and document fund expenditures

Deadlines

  • 2033-06-30: The provision allowing agencies to expend their own funds becomes inoperative. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)
  • 2034-01-01: The provisions are fully repealed and the letter of no prejudice mechanism will no longer be available. (Affected agencies: City, County, Special District, Joint Powers Authority)

Additional Requirements

  • Submission of a letter of no prejudice application to the Secretary of Transportation
  • Compliance with all stipulated reimbursement conditions
  • Timely expenditure of funds prior to the inoperative date
  • Maintenance of documentation to support reimbursement claims

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Public Finance (primary) - The change directly alters funding mechanisms and reimbursement structures, making expertise in public finance essential for managing these new options.
  • Municipal (primary) - Cities, counties, and other local entities (e.g., Special Districts and Joint Powers Authorities) are likely to use this provision, requiring guidance on municipal legal and operational issues.

Summary:
The legislative change increases the cap on administrative expenditures from 2.5% to 5% of the bond proceeds under the Safe, Reliable, High-Speed Passenger Train Bond Act for the 21st Century. This amendment adjusts the allocation formula used for planning, engineering, and capital costs associated with high-speed rail development. It is a state-level financing issue and does not directly impact any of the designated local public agency types.

Summary:
The legislative change requires the Department of Transportation to compile and report, on an annual basis beginning in 2025, information and analyses on the performance of its zero‐emission vehicle fleet (including details on charging and refueling facilities), with these reporting provisions set to expire on January 1, 2036. As a result, none of the local public agencies (e.g., School District, Charter School, County Office of Education, Community College, City, County, Special District, or Joint Powers Authority) are directly impacted.

Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 12.

Summary:
The legislative change revises the funding allocation for the Reconnecting Communities: Highways to Boulevards Pilot Program. Under existing law, 25% of funds were set aside for planning and 75% for implementation. The new provision allows up to 25% for planning with the balance for implementation. This adjustment affects how project sponsors, typically large public entities, plan and structure their proposals, but it does not directly impose new compliance obligations on specific local public agencies.

Practice Group: Governance

Substantive Change
Trailer Bill Sections: SEC. 7., SEC. 8., SEC. 9., SEC. 10., SEC. 11.

Summary:
The legislative change expands the responsibilities and oversight functions of the High-Speed Rail Authority’s Inspector General. It imposes new duties to review and investigate compliance with contract provisions on public and private entities receiving high-speed rail project funds, requires the inclusion of specific inspector general access provisions in all contracts executed on or after July 1, 2024, extends whistleblower protections to contractor employees, and revises budget notification protocols. These changes affect a state-level rail project and its contracting process; no local public agency (e.g., School District, Charter School, County Office of Education, Community College, City, County, Special District, or Joint Powers Authority) is directly impacted.

Local Agencies Impacted

No local agencies identified.

Impact Description:
No direct impact on local agencies.

Key Action Items

  • Review and update all contract templates to incorporate the required inspector general access provisions effective July 1, 2024.
  • Advise contractor employees and involved parties on the extended whistleblower protections and update internal policies accordingly.
  • Monitor the changes in budget notification protocols and adjust compliance processes to ensure timely disclosure to legislative budget committees.

Deadlines

  • 2024-07-01: All new contracts and contract amendments expending high-speed rail project funds must include the mandated inspector general access provisions.

Additional Requirements

  • All contracts executed on or after July 1, 2024 must include provisions granting the inspector general full access to records and property necessary for oversight.
  • Establish clear procedures for periodic review or investigation of adherence to contract provisions, including for public and private entities receiving project funds.

Relevant Practice Groups

  • Governance (primary) - The amendment alters oversight functions and contract compliance review, requiring counsel on governance and regulatory adherence for entities managing large-scale state projects.
  • Labor and Employment (secondary) - Extending whistleblower protections to contractor employees necessitates review and advice on employment law implications.
  • Public Finance (secondary) - Revisions to budget notification requirements to legislative budget committees call for guidance on fiscal transparency and public finance practices.
